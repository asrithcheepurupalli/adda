# netflix, uninterrupted

*a concept study from made. by ac · july 2026 · the clickable mock lives in `netflix-mock.html`*

Fifteen ad placements across three screens. Six on mobile, five on TV, four on desktop. Not one of them stops the story, sits above the fold, or touches the hero. We designed each one around attention that already exists inside the app and currently earns nothing.

## 1. Why we made this

- **Netflix India runs zero ads today.** Every plan is ad free: Mobile at ₹149, Basic at ₹199, Standard at ₹499, Premium at ₹649 a month. India is one of the only major Netflix markets earning nothing from advertising.
- **Globally, ads are Netflix's fastest growing business.** The ad tier crossed **250 million monthly viewers** in May 2026, up from 190 million in November. It takes more than 60% of new signups wherever it exists, and Netflix expects to roughly double ad revenue to about **3 billion dollars this year**, with a stated path to 9 billion by 2030. Over 4,000 advertisers already, up 70% in a year.
- **Indian viewers do not hate ads. They hate interruption.** Hotstar, YouTube, Zee5 and SonyLIV all run ads and people watch anyway. What Netflix owns in India is the feeling of never being interrupted, and that is worth protecting.

So the pitch is simple. Netflix does not need Hotstar's midroll to earn in India. There is a whole layer of quiet moments inside the app, pauses, countdowns, progress bars, idle screens, where attention is high and the story is untouched. That layer is currently worth ₹0.

## 2. Our three rules

1. **Never stop the story.** No preroll, no midroll, nothing over a playing frame. If content is playing, no ad exists.
2. **Never touch the hero.** The billboard and the top rows stay 100% editorial. Discovery trust is the product.
3. **Only borrow attention that already exists.** Every placement lives in a moment where the viewer is already waiting, pausing or browsing. We monetize dead time, we never create it.

## 3. Mobile · P1 to P6 · where India watches

### P1 · Pause Spot
The viewer pressed pause, so the story stopped and they stopped it. We wait three seconds so accidental pauses never see anything, then a brand card settles beside the untouched frame. Touch anything and it vanishes. Hulu proved the recall on this format and Netflix already runs it in ad tier markets. We bring it to India without the tier.
Around 550M impressions a month · ₹180 CPM · food delivery, QSR, drinks.

### P2 · Binge Bridge
The countdown between episodes is the most predictable attention window in streaming and today it sits empty. The sponsor rests beside the timer, the next episode still starts exactly on schedule, and Play now still skips instantly. Copy that knows the moment, like "Episode 5 already? Dinner is on us", reads as a wink. Capped at one in every three episodes.
Around 400M impressions a month · ₹220 CPM · food, commerce, late night offers.

### P3 · Sponsored Collection
Not a banner. A curated row of real Netflix titles that a brand presents, styled like every editorial row, always below the fold. Think "Friday Night Thrillers, presented by CRED". The brand buys the mood, Netflix keeps full control of the titles. Closer to a partnership than an ad, which is why it prices like one.
Weekly row sponsorship · ₹8 to 12 Cr a month across devices · fintech, premium D2C, auto.

### P4 · Search Spotlight
One clearly labelled sponsored tile at the very end of the results, never above anything organic. The best version promotes content the brand is already attached to, so even the ad is still a show. One per query, capped.
Around 90M impressions a month · ₹250 CPM · title tie ins and co marketing.

### P5 · Download Break
India downloads more Netflix than anywhere else. While a download runs, the viewer is literally watching a progress bar, so one card under the queue fills genuinely dead time. A telecom saying "this download is on us" feels less like an ad and more like a favour. Mobile only, this surface exists nowhere else.
Around 200M impressions a month · ₹120 CPM · telecom, UPI, fintech.

### P6 · New & Hot Preview
New & Hot is already a trailer feed, promotional by design. One film grade branded spot for every eight organic cards, sound off, skipped with a flick. The only surface where a video ad is native to what people came to do. The creative bar stays theatrical.
Around 180M impressions a month · ₹300 CPM · entertainment tie ins, lifestyle.

## 4. TV · T1 to T5 · the premium screen

TV is watched together, more than two pairs of eyes per screen on average, and the QR code moves every transaction to the phone so the television never becomes a checkout.

### T1 · Pause Spot, living room edition
A TV pause usually means someone actually left the couch. Chai, doorbell, kids. The card sits beside the untouched 4K frame, the QR does the work on the phone. Biggest screen in the house, priced like it.
Around 280M impressions a month · ₹350 CPM · food delivery, auto, consumer durables.

### T2 · Binge Bridge, family edition
The gap between episodes is when the room negotiates dinner, so the sponsor talks to the room. Family sized offers, weekend plans. The episode still plays exactly on time.
Around 220M impressions a month · ₹380 CPM · food, family commerce, travel.

### T3 · Still Watching Spot · only on TV
The one moment where Netflix itself stops the show, the "are you still watching?" prompt. The viewer is asleep or gone, so an ad here cannot interrupt anyone. Netflix already did the interrupting. A card that says "fell asleep mid binge? tomorrow's coffee is on us" is witty, kind and harmless by construction.
Around 45M impressions a month · ₹300 CPM · coffee, breakfast, morning brands.

### T4 · Sponsored Collection, ten foot edition
The same brand presented row rebuilt for remotes. Landscape tiles, a visible focus ring. TV browsing is the longest and most deliberate kind, a room deciding together, so good curation genuinely helps the evening.
Weekly sponsorship · about 1.5x the mobile row · fintech, auto, premium D2C.

### T5 · Ambient Gallery · only on TV, and our favourite
When a TV sits idle, Netflix drifts into gallery style title art. Nobody is presenting that wall today. A premium brand takes it with one quiet chip on full bleed artwork, the way brands sponsor exhibitions. Nothing is interrupted because nothing is playing. The most elegant money in this whole deck.
Monthly gallery sponsorship · ₹2 to 4 Cr a month · jewellery, watches, auto, luxury.

## 5. Desktop · D1 to D4 · the conversion screen

Smallest reach in India, but the only Netflix screen with a cursor. Every card here is one honest click from a landing page or a filled cart, and that is the conversions proof this whole idea needs.

### D1 · Pause Spot, clickable
No QR hop. The pause card is a real link, one click and the cart is filled. Any click on the player or any key dismisses it. The only placement with a true measurable click.
Around 70M impressions a month · ₹200 CPM plus CPC upside · D2C, quick commerce, SaaS.

### D2 · Binge Bridge, click to copy
The promo code copies to the clipboard on click, which hands Netflix and the brand a clean attribution loop, impression to copy to redemption. If we had to pick one placement for the first case study on conversions, this is it.
Around 55M impressions a month · ₹240 CPM.

### D3 · Sponsored Collection, hover previews
Every rule holds, real titles, row three or lower, hero untouched, plus the desktop superpower. Hover any tile in the sponsored row and its preview plays exactly like the editorial rows. The brand buys the label, never the behaviour. Sold as one bundle with the mobile and TV rows.

### D4 · Search Spotlight, typed intent
A five column grid gives the sponsored tile plenty of organic company. Strictly last, clearly labelled, and a click opens a new tab only when the viewer explicitly asks.
Around 25M impressions a month · ₹280 CPM.

## 6. Everybody wins

| | what they get |
|---|---|
| **Netflix** | A new revenue line in a market earning nothing from ads today, without breaking the promise that defines the brand here. And an option down the road: these placements could power a ₹99 to 129 "Supported" plan for the next hundred million price sensitive viewers. |
| **Brands** | Netflix grade context, 4K, big screens, an audience mixing affluence and youth, available nowhere else in Indian streaming. Moments instead of spray, genre, time of day, binge depth. |
| **Viewers** | The show never stops. The home screen never changes above the fold. Maybe a cheaper plan appears. The ads they do meet are curation, offers and trailers, the tolerable kind. |

## 7. What it could be worth

Our working assumptions: 12 to 15 million Indian subscribers, about 1.5 sessions a day, roughly 2.4 pauses and 1.8 episode transitions per session, and a 70 / 22 / 8 device split across mobile, TV and desktop.

| placement | screens | impressions a month | CPM range (₹) | monthly potential |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Pause Spot | mobile, TV, desktop | ~900M | 180 to 350 | ₹22 Cr |
| Binge Bridge | mobile, TV, desktop | ~675M | 220 to 380 | ₹18 Cr |
| Sponsored Collection | bundle across devices | sponsorship | · | ₹10 to 14 Cr |
| Search Spotlight | mobile, desktop | ~115M | 250 to 280 | ₹3 Cr |
| Download Break | mobile only | ~200M | 120 | ₹2.4 Cr |
| New & Hot Preview | mobile | ~180M | 300 | ₹5.4 Cr |
| Still Watching Spot | TV only | ~45M | 300 | ₹1.4 Cr |
| Ambient Gallery | TV only | sponsorship | · | ₹2 to 4 Cr |

**Indicatively ₹60 to 75 Cr a month, roughly 85 to 105 million dollars a year, before any ad supported tier exists and with zero seconds of playback interrupted.** The numbers are directional. For the real conversation we would rebuild them from Netflix's own engagement data, and we would love to.

## 8. What we do next

1. Put the mock in front of 20 to 30 Netflix India users and ask one question: did anything feel intrusive?
2. Rebuild the sizing from real benchmarks, BARC and Comscore India OTT data, Hotstar rate cards.
3. Line up five launch partner brands per placement. Telecom, food delivery, fintech, D2C.
4. Wrap it in a ten slide deck around the clickable mock and take it to Netflix India partnerships.
